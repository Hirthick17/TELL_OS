Behavioural Research

The Psychology Behind
These Problems

Why do merchants stick to broken systems? Understanding the human behaviour underneath the pain is what makes product design and positioning land. These are the 7 mental models at play.

01
Cognitive Trigger: Loss Aversion
The Fear of Missing a Sale
The reason merchants check WhatsApp every 10 minutes isn't excitement — it's fear. Missing an order message means losing a sale. This hyper-vigilance creates compulsive checking behaviour that keeps them trapped in WhatsApp even when it fails them as a system.
🪝 Product Hook: Frame the daily summary and order alerts as "never miss a sale again" — not as automation. Loss prevention resonates 2.5× stronger than efficiency gains.
02
Cognitive Trigger: Illusion of Control
Colour-Coded Labels as False Control
Merchants build elaborate label systems ("New Inquiry" → "Payment Awaited" → "Packed") because it feels like a process. It isn't — it's a simulation of control. The system collapses at volume, but the effort invested creates the sunk cost fallacy: "I built this system, it must work."
🪝 Product Hook: Don't call their system broken. Say "you've already built the logic — we just run it automatically so you don't have to remember." Validate first, then replace.
03
Cognitive Trigger: Tool Fatigue
The New Tool Resistance
Merchants have already tried 2–3 tools that promised to solve this and didn't. They've paid for platforms they barely used. This creates a strong anti-adoption bias. "Another app" is not a selling proposition — it triggers memory of past failures and switching costs.
🪝 Product Hook: "You don't need another app. Just send a WhatsApp message." The zero-install, familiar-interface approach is the only way past this barrier.
04
Cognitive Trigger: Status Identity
The "Real Business" Aspiration
Instagram and WhatsApp sellers often feel like they're not running a "real" business because they don't have systems. They see Shopify brands as more legitimate. This creates hidden aspiration — they want to operate professionally without the complexity of professional tools.
🪝 Product Hook: "Run your business like a pro, from WhatsApp." The status aspiration — professionalism without complexity — is a powerful motivator for this segment.
05
Cognitive Trigger: Present Bias
"I'll Fix It When I Scale"
Merchants consistently defer systematisation until some future threshold ("when I hit 50 orders a day, I'll get a proper system"). The irony: the moment they need the system is exactly when they have least time to set it up. Present bias keeps them under-systematised indefinitely.
🪝 Product Hook: Trigger urgency at exactly the moment of pain. If they just said "it breaks when volume goes up" — that's the moment to show how 5 minutes now prevents breakdown at scale.
06
Cognitive Trigger: Proximity Comfort
WhatsApp as Trusted Territory
WhatsApp is where Indian merchants live — family, friends, customers, suppliers. It's trusted, familiar, and already habitual. Any new tool faces the fundamental switching problem: it asks them to go somewhere they don't naturally go. WhatsApp doesn't have this problem.
🪝 Product Hook: The product IS WhatsApp — don't frame it as integrating with WhatsApp. The channel is the product. This is the core positioning advantage.
07
Cognitive Trigger: Memory Overload
Memory as Infrastructure
Sellers carry a terrifying amount of business state in their heads: which customer ordered what, which item is low, what price they quoted in which conversation. When they describe their process, they use the word "I remember." Memory IS their system — and it's non-transferable, non-scalable, and fragile.
🪝 Product Hook: "Stop remembering. Start asking." Externalise their memory into a queryable system. The relief of offloading cognitive load is deeply emotional — not rational.
